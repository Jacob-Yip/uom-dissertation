\section*{Abstract}
% Add to table of contents manually
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}
While current machine learning typically relies on independent and single-model architectures~\cite{zhao2023survey}, ensemble learning leverages a ``committee'' of learners to achieve superior predictive accuracy. This dissertation investigates the theoretical and empirical benefits of ensemble learning, with a specific focus on Negative Correlation Learning (NCL). Through a series of mathematical derivations and experiments, we demonstrate that an ensemble model consistently outperforms a single Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP). 

A central theme of this work is the management of diversity, which is the degree to which base learners capture distinct patterns in the data. We explore the ``double-edged sword'' of diversity: while controlled diversity reduces ensemble risk, unmanaged diversity can degrade performance. This research further extends the NCL framework by integrating it with NeuroEvolution of Augmenting Topologies (NEAT), enabling an analysis of architectural diversity alongside weight-based diversity. 

Key achievements include the empirical validation of the Ambiguity Decomposition theorem and the proposal of a recursive ensemble framework that utilises optimised structural arrangements and neural-network aggregators. Furthermore, this project introduces a novel algorithm designed to convert dynamic NEAT-based neural networks into a GPU-runnable format for inference, providing a significant computational performance boost. Our findings confirm that when diversity and aggregation are properly aligned, ensemble models offer higher accuracy and greater resource efficiency than single-model approaches.
